The Problem: Aflatoxin in Maize

Across many regions of the Global South, smallholder farmers face challenges that extend far beyond crop production. One significant problem is the contamination of staple crops such as maize by aflatoxins — toxic compounds produced by fungi including Aspergillus flavus. Exposure can cause liver damage, immune suppression, and impaired child development, while contaminated crops may fail food-safety standards needed for local markets and trade.

A major cause of contamination is excessive moisture during drying and storage. Research suggests maize should be dried to roughly 14% moisture content or below to substantially reduce fungal growth. Yet many farmers rely on traditional open-air drying, leaving crops vulnerable to rain, humidity, pests, and contamination.

Working alongside Bernard and local engineers and scientists in Kenya, the SOLARSAFE project is developing low-cost, solar-powered drying containers that help farmers reach safer moisture levels before long-term storage — reducing crop losses, improving food safety, and strengthening economic resilience.

Our Approach: Material Selection

Our team investigated how the choice of plastic affects the performance, hygiene, and durability of the drying enclosure. The ideal bag material must:

Withstands NaOCl cleaning Reduces aflatoxin trapping Supports moisture control Transparent Low cost Durable

Materials shortlisted

PVC (the material SOLARSAFE currently uses — retained as control), polypropylene (PP), and nylon — all transparent, food-safe, durable, widely available, and with contrasting gas/moisture permeability. LDPE was eliminated based on literature evidence of Aspergillus-related accumulation, and PELA was excluded because its opacity is incompatible with the transparent drying system.

Experiments

Experiment 1 — Sodium Hypochlorite Exposure (ATR-FTIR & Microscopy)

Sodium hypochlorite is a low-cost disinfectant already widely available in Kenya. We exposed PP, PVC, and nylon samples to NaOCl solutions (3%, 5%, and 5% with heat), then assessed chemical degradation with ATR-FTIR spectroscopy and surface damage with optical microscopy — testing whether repeated cleaning is a feasible, affordable hygiene strategy.

Experiment 2 — Moisture & Headspace Monitoring

We measured moisture levels and CO₂ concentrations inside maize-filled bags of each material. These indicators characterise the internal storage environment and help identify conditions that may favour microbial activity.

Key Findings

PP & PVC
No clear chemical degradation after NaOCl exposure
PP & Nylon
Highest moisture retention in bag tests
Nylon
Inconclusive ATR results — needs further testing

Recommendation

PVC is the strongest current candidate, combining chemical stability, transparency, availability, and acceptable moisture performance — with PP retained as a strong comparator. Further testing of nylon, PE, and PET is needed before a final material certification. This work is a first-stage material screen, not a final certification.

Project Management & Reflection

Team structure

The wider team split into mechanics and materials sub-teams in week one. Within the materials pair, Kunran focused on aflatoxin research, material selection, and procurement; Mirha focused on sodium hypochlorite research, experimental design, and risk assessment — with both sharing outreach, literature review, and the end-to-end experimental pipeline.

Expert consultation

Rich Stutt guided us to plant-epidemiology literature; Gideon Darko advised on moisture measurement and aflatoxin quantification; Professor Eugene Terentjev supervised the chemical exposure work in the plastics lab; and coordinators Lara and Tom steered the materials focus and microscale observation.

Challenges & adaptations

  • Procurement delays — engineering-grade samples were slow to arrive, so we adapted using commercial bags made of the same target materials (PP, PVC, nylon).
  • Lab access — over 20 outreach emails were needed before securing supervised access to the plastics lab.
  • Aflatoxin complexity — direct fungal testing needs specialist equipment and time, so it was treated as a research component while experiments focused on durability and chemical resistance.
Key lessons: narrow the scope early, contact experts quickly, and keep contingency plans for procurement and lab access.

Future Exploration

  • Multi-sensor mapping of oxygen and moisture distribution inside bags over time.
  • Longer NaOCl exposure with replicates, improved ATR-FTIR contact, and profilometry for surface roughness.
  • UV, heat, and mechanical ageing to represent field use; folding, puncture, and tensile testing after chemical exposure.
  • Testing PE and PET as further candidates; small PVC and PP prototype bags trialled with maize under sunlight.
  • Aflasafe and other biocontrol options as a pre-harvest complement; field validation in Kenya before final material adoption.

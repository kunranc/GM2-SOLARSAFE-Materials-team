01 · Improving crop safety

Aflatoxin contamination affects health, food safety, and farmer income.

Across many regions of the Global South, smallholder farmers face challenges that extend far beyond crop production itself. One significant problem is contamination of staple crops such as maize by aflatoxins, toxic compounds produced by fungi including Aspergillus flavus.

Why aflatoxins matter

  • ⚕Exposure to aflatoxins can cause serious health problems in humans and livestock.
  • 🌽Contaminated crops may fail to meet food safety standards for local markets and international trade.
  • 💷Unsafe grain can create economic consequences for farming communities through crop losses and reduced market access.
  • 🌧Traditional open-air drying leaves crops vulnerable to rain, humidity, pests, and contamination.

The role of moisture

A major cause of aflatoxin contamination is excessive moisture during drying and storage. Research suggests that maize should be dried to approximately 14% moisture content or below to substantially reduce fungal growth and aflatoxin production.

Many farmers continue to rely on open-air drying methods. This creates a practical need for low-cost systems that provide a more controlled drying environment before long-term storage.

02 · SolarSafe approach

SolarSafe aims to make drying safer, more controlled, and more useful for farmers.

Working alongside Bernard and local engineers and scientists in Kenya, SolarSafe seeks to address aflatoxin risk through improved crop drying and storage technologies.

Controlled drying

Low-cost solar-powered containers

SolarSafe aims to help farmers achieve safer moisture levels before long-term storage using low-cost solar-powered drying containers.

Food safety

Reducing contamination risk

A more controlled drying environment has the potential to reduce crop losses and improve food safety by limiting conditions that favour fungal growth.

Economic resilience

Supporting farming communities

Safer drying and storage can help farmers protect harvest value and improve resilience within farming communities.

Our contribution

Our team focused on understanding how material selection influences the performance and durability of SolarSafe drying systems. We investigated whether different plastic materials could affect environmental conditions within the container that are associated with fungal growth. In particular, moisture levels and carbon dioxide concentrations provide indicators of the internal storage environment and can help identify conditions that may favour microbial activity.

03 · Challenges and adaptations

Practical constraints shaped the final experimental route.

The project required fast adaptation because the available time, laboratory access, and material procurement did not always match the original experimental ambition.

Procurement delays

Engineering-grade plastic materials were slow to arrive due to their specialised nature, which conflicted with the project deadline. The team adapted by using plastic bags made from the same target materials — PP, PVC, and nylon — to preserve the purpose of the experiment.

Laboratory access

Arranging laboratory access and supervision for chemical exposure testing was another challenge. The team conducted extensive outreach, including more than 20 emails to different departments, laboratories, and academics, before securing support in the plastics lab.

Aflatoxin complexity

Directly testing aflatoxin production or fungal adhesion would have required specialist equipment, safety procedures, and more time than the project allowed. Therefore, this area was treated primarily as a research component while experimental work focused on material durability and chemical resistance.

04 · Project management and team reflection

The team narrowed a broad problem into a focused materials investigation.

The project management strategy was based on dividing work clearly while maintaining flexibility. In the first week, the wider team split into mechanics and materials sub-teams because different members wanted to explore different aspects of SolarSafe.

1

Team structure

The smaller materials team meant that each person had to take on more responsibility, while also gaining wider exposure to literature review, experimental design, procurement, risk assessment, and laboratory preparation.

2

Selecting the research focus

The team initially considered heating, drying conditions, oxygen and moisture control, fungal contamination, and bag materials. After discussion with Benard and review of the post-harvest process, the team settled on bag materials as a practical and relevant focus.

3

Expert consultation

Rich Stutt guided the team towards relevant plant epidemiology literature. Gideon Darko advised on possible experimental set-ups, including moisture measurement using a John Deere device and aflatoxin quantification using Reveal Q. Eugene Terentjev supported the plastics lab work and helped make chemical exposure testing more realistic.

4

Shared responsibilities

Kunran focused on aflatoxin research, material selection, and procurement searches. Mirha focused on sodium hypochlorite research, experimental design, and risk assessments. Both members contributed to outreach, literature review, and end-to-end experimental pipelines.

05 · Future exploration

Future work could expand the material range and test the internal environment in more detail.

If more time had been available, the project could have extended beyond chemical exposure testing and examined more realistic internal conditions inside drying bags.

Oxygen and moisture distribution

Future work could use multiple sensors to compare how different materials affect oxygen and moisture distribution inside the bags over time.

Aflasafe pathway

Aflasafe could be explored as a potential future biocontrol strategy for Kenya, particularly for preventing pre-harvest infection before the drying stage.

More candidate materials

Other materials, such as PE and PET, would also be valuable candidates for testing.

The project demonstrated the importance of narrowing the scope early, contacting experts quickly, and preparing contingency plans for procurement and laboratory access.

06 · Key lesson

Simple engineering decisions can support safer crop drying technologies.

By contributing evidence-based material selection to SolarSafe, the work supports the development of safer crop drying technologies for smallholder farmers. More broadly, it highlights how material selection can address challenges at the intersection of food security, public health, and sustainable development.

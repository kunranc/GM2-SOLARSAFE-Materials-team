# Q1. Context: Improving Crop Safety for Smallholder Farmers in Kenya

Across many regions of the Global South, smallholder farmers face challenges that extend far beyond crop production itself. One significant problem is the contamination of staple crops such as maize by aflatoxins, toxic compounds produced by fungi including Aspergillus flavus. Exposure to aflatoxins can cause serious health problems in humans and livestock, while contaminated crops may fail to meet food safety standards required for local markets and international trade. As a result, aflatoxin contamination can have health, environmental, and economic consequences for farming communities.

Working alongside Bernard and local engineers and scientists in Kenya, the SolarSafe project seeks to address this challenge through improved crop drying and storage technologies. A major cause of aflatoxin contamination is excessive moisture during drying and storage. Research suggests that maize should be dried to approximately 14% moisture content or below to substantially reduce fungal growth and aflatoxin production. However, many farmers continue to rely on traditional open-air drying methods, which leave crops vulnerable to rain, humidity, pests, and contamination.

SolarSafe aims to develop low-cost solar powered drying containers that help farmers achieve safer moisture levels before long-term storage. By providing a more controlled drying environment, these containers have the potential to reduce crop losses, improve food safety, and increase economic resilience within farming communities.

Our team's contribution focused on understanding how material selection influences the performance and durability of such drying systems. We investigated whether different plastic materials could affect environmental conditions within the container that are associated with fungal growth. In particular, we measured moisture levels and carbon dioxide concentrations, which provide indicators of the internal storage environment and can help identify conditions that may favour microbial activity.

Alongside improving conditions inside the drying container, we also investigated ways to protect crops from contamination originating in the external environment. We tested several plastic materials by exposing them to sodium hypochlorite, a low-cost disinfectant already widely available in Kenya, to determine which materials could best tolerate repeated cleaning. This allowed us to assess whether regular disinfection could be a feasible and affordable method of maintaining hygiene, reducing microbial contamination, and improving the long-term durability of the drying containers used by farmers.

Although our study examined only three flexible plastic materials, the experimental methods we developed can be readily extended to evaluate a much wider range of candidate materials. Our results demonstrate that material choice can influence both the internal conditions experienced by stored maize and the durability of the drying container itself.

By contributing evidence-based material selection to the SolarSafe project, our work supports the development of safer crop drying technologies for smallholder farmers. More broadly, it highlights how relatively simple engineering decisions can help address complex challenges at the intersection of food security, public health, and sustainable development.

# Q2. Alignment with Sustainable Development Goals and Digital Development Principles

The SolarSafe project aligns strongly with several United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), particularly SDG 2 (Zero Hunger), SDG 3 (Good Health and Well-being), SDG 8 (Decent Work and Economic Growth), and SDG 12 (Responsible Consumption and Production).

The most direct connection is with SDG 2, which aims to improve food security and agricultural productivity. Aflatoxin contamination reduces the quantity of safe food available to households and can lead to significant post-harvest losses. By helping farmers dry maize more effectively and reduce fungal growth during storage, SolarSafe contributes to preserving food supplies and improving agricultural outcomes.

The project also supports SDG 3. Aflatoxins are associated with a range of health risks, including liver disease, immune suppression, and developmental impacts in children. Reducing contamination therefore provides benefits beyond agriculture by improving public health outcomes. Technologies that prevent contamination before it occurs are particularly valuable because they reduce risks at the source rather than relying solely on later testing and intervention.

Economic resilience is another important consideration. Smallholder farmers often depend on maize sales for household income. Contaminated grain may be rejected by buyers or sold at reduced prices. By supporting safer storage and drying practices, SolarSafe may help farmers access markets more reliably and reduce avoidable financial losses, contributing to SDG 8.

Our materials-focused contribution also reflects SDG 12. Material selection influences durability, maintenance requirements, and product lifespan. Evaluating resistance to sodium hypochlorite cleaning allows designers to identify materials that can be reused repeatedly rather than replaced frequently. This improves resource efficiency and reduces waste generation over time.

Beyond the SDGs, the project aligns with several UNICEF Principles for Digital Development. Although SolarSafe is primarily a physical engineering intervention rather than a digital platform, many of the principles remain relevant. The project was designed with the user in mind, focusing on the practical realities faced by Kenyan smallholder farmers. Cost, availability of materials, maintenance requirements, and local access to disinfectants were all considered during experimental design.

The project also demonstrates the principle of building for sustainability. Rather than proposing highly specialised equipment, SolarSafe seeks to utilise affordable materials and locally available resources that could realistically be adopted and maintained within existing agricultural systems.

Another important consideration is collaboration. The project involved close engagement with academics, agricultural scientists, materials specialists, and local stakeholders. This multidisciplinary approach improved both the technical quality and practical relevance of the work.

Finally, the project highlights the importance of appropriate technology in international development. Effective solutions are not necessarily the most technologically advanced but those that best meet user needs within local economic and environmental constraints. By focusing on affordability, durability, and food safety, SolarSafe aims to deliver meaningful impact through engineering choices that are realistic, scalable, and grounded in the needs of farming communities.

# Q3. Project Management and Team Reflection

## Project Management

### Team Structure and Division of Work

Our project management strategy was based on dividing work clearly while maintaining flexibility. In the first week, the team split into two sub-teams because different members wanted to explore different aspects of SOLARSAFE (mechanics and materials). This made the work more efficient, as each pair could focus deeply on one direction. Within the materials team, the smaller group size meant that each person had to take on more responsibility, but it also gave both members wider exposure to literature review, experimental design, procurement, risk assessment, and laboratory preparation. We also supported each other when one person was unavailable, which helped to keep the project moving.

### Selecting the Research Focus

A major part of the project was question selection. Initially, there were many possible directions, including heating, drying conditions, oxygen and moisture control, fungal contamination, and bag materials. After we discussed with Benard, who is also working on solving this problem himself, we reconsidered what would be the best use of our limited time. We wanted to choose a topic that was not only technically interesting but also practically relevant to the Kenyan context. After reviewing the post-harvest process and comparing possible areas of contribution, we finally settled on the topic of bag materials.

### Expert Consultation and External Support

Reaching out to experts was especially useful because it gave us practical direction rather than only general feedback. Rich Stutt helped guide us towards relevant plant epidemiology literature, including comparable work on Aspergillus flavus. Gideon Darko gave advice on possible experimental set-ups, including moisture measurement using a John Deere device and aflatoxin quantification using Reveal Q. Eugene Terentjev’s support was important for the experimental side, as he agreed to supervise us in the plastics lab and helped make the chemical exposure testing more realistic. We also took useful advice from the project coordinators: Lara first encouraged us to think about the materials side, while Tom advised us to connect with someone at the plastics lab to observe the bag at the microscale.

### Material Selection

Material selection was guided by SolarSafe's operational requirements and findings from the literature. PELA and LDPE were initially considered but were excluded due to practical limitations. PELA, despite its strong barrier properties, is opaque and incompatible with SolarSafe's transparent drying system, while LDPE has been associated with higher aflatoxin accumulation in previous studies. PVC was retained as a control as it is the material currently used by SolarSafe. Polypropylene (PP) and nylon were selected as candidate materials because they are transparent, food-safe, durable, widely available, and exhibit different gas and moisture permeability characteristics, allowing their influence on the drying environment to be investigated.

### Allocation of Responsibilities

Responsibilities were clearly divided. Kunran focused on aflatoxin research, material selection, and procurement searches, while Mirha focused on sodium hypochlorite research, experimental design, and risk assessments. However, both members contributed to outreach, literature review, and end-to-end experimental pipelines. This ensured that knowledge was shared rather than isolated.

## Challenges and Adaptations

### Procurement Delays

One major challenge was procurement delays. Engineering-grade plastic materials were slow to arrive due to their specialised nature, which conflicted with the project deadline. To avoid losing experimental time, we quickly adapted by using plastic bags made from the same target materials—PP, PVC, and nylon. This preserved the purpose of the experiment while allowing more time for testing.

### Laboratory Access and Supervision

A second challenge was arranging laboratory access and supervision for chemical exposure testing. We conducted extensive outreach, including more than 20 emails to different departments, laboratories, and academics. Eventually, we secured support from Professor Eugene in the plastics lab, which allowed us to conduct the work under proper supervision and follow standard laboratory procedures.

### Complexity of Aflatoxin Research

A third challenge was the complexity of aflatoxin research. Directly testing aflatoxin production or fungal adhesion would have required specialist equipment, safety procedures, and more time than the project allowed. Therefore, we treated this area primarily as a research component while focusing the experimental work on material durability and chemical resistance.

## Future Exploration

If more time had been available, we would have extended the project beyond chemical exposure testing. In particular, we would have tested oxygen and moisture distribution inside the bags using multiple sensors to compare how different materials affect internal conditions over time. We would also have explored Aflasafe as a potential future biocontrol strategy for Kenya, particularly for preventing pre-harvest infection before the drying stage. Other materials, such as PE and PET, would also be valuable candidates for testing.

## Key Lessons Learned

Overall, the project demonstrated the importance of narrowing the scope early, contacting experts quickly, and preparing contingency plans for procurement and laboratory access. Despite practical constraints, the team reached an evidence-based conclusion that was both scientifically grounded and practically useful, with the potential to support future improvements in drying-bag design.
